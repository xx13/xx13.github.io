What is Ask 'Em?

Ask ‘Em is a new way to start a dialogue with a vendor without having to jump all the way into their typical sales process. Ask ‘Em was designed to allow you to get your initial specific questions answered by a set of vendors with minimal time, effort and friction.

Using Ask ‘Em, you are able to remain anonymous and are able to put time limits on how long it should take you to digest vendors’ answers to your questions. After using Ask ‘Em, you should be able to make a far more informed decision about whether you think it makes sense to take a meeting or a phone call with one or more of the vendors you heard back from.

How it works?

1. Add vendors to your list

Use the map to discover categories and companies that are relevant to you. To include a company to receive your questions, add them to your list.

If there is a vendor that you would like to include in your Ask ‘Em that you do not see on the map, just let us know by clicking ‘Add a company to the map’ from the menu. While we’re busy adding that company, you can still continue with your Ask ‘Em to the other companies in your list.

2. Get started

There are two ways to start your Ask ’Em. You can click an icon in the search section at the top of the page. The other way is to click a button in a company info block on the left.

3. Name your Ask 'Em

Your Ask ‘Em name will not be seen by anyone else. This is just a name to use to represent your set of questions. Company name is how you will appear to the vendors who receive your questions.

You can choose to provide your company name or you can choose to provide an anonymous description of your company (e.g. “Tier 1 Food & Beverage CPG Company”), which will help the vendor to be able to tailor their answers to your specific industry. Your name, title, email or any other contact information will never be shared with the vendor unless you explicitly choose to do so.

4. Give vendors guidance

This is your chance to provide some context to your questions to help guide the vendors to provide you with the most relevant answers possible. We highly recommend included information around:
• Are you using a similar solution today?
• How familiar are you with the category? Newbie? Expert? Somewhere in between?
• Where are you in your process/search? Are you likely to make a purchasing decision in the next 3 months? 6 months? 12 - 18 months? Or just at the earliest stages of kicking the tires?

5. List your questions with time limits

This is where you are able to add your specific questions that you’d like to invite the vendors that you have selected to answer. While people have used Ask ‘Em for everything from the most preliminary RFIs to very detailed RFPs, a good rule of thumb is to focus on which questions you need answered to understand if the category is relevant and interesting to you and if it makes sense for you to take a meeting or phone call w/ the company that is answering the questions.

The time limits state the most amount of time you are willing to spend reading a vendor’s answer to that particular question. Ask ‘Em will translate your time limits into character limits for the vendors.

6. Review & submit

Congrats! Your work here is done. Now its time for these vendors to take a look at your guidance and questions and make sure they are a relevant fit. If they believe they are, they will get the right set of folks on their team to get you answers to your questions. Please be patient as this process can take anywhere from a few days to a week or two.

Take a look at everything in one place. If you see anything you want to change, just click the Back button.

Providing Guidance

Guidance is your opportunity to give the vendors context to your questions. Providing this guidance is crucial to enabling these vendors to give you the most relevant responses possible. Your guidance should including the following:

  1. 1. Stage and timing — Provide context as to where you are in a purchasing process. Examples below:

    • “We’re just first trying to gain an understanding of what this category is. As such,we do not have a formal budget, process or timeline in place as of yet. However,if we do uncover that this could drive significant value, we would absolutely take steps to go down a more deliberate purchase evaluation process”
    • “We are highly interested in this category as we believe we can derive significant value from implemented such a solution. That being said, we are at the beginning stages of our evaluation process. We will likely make a purchasing decision in the next 6-9 months.”
    • “We are likely to make a purchase in the next 3-6 months and are ensuring we are aware of all vendors in this space and narrowing it down to a short-list”
  2. 2. Your knowledge level — Help the vendor understand your knowledge level so they know whether they should focus on introducing you to the most basic concepts, should jump to the most intricate and cutting edge details or somewhere in between.
  3. 3. Existing solution/provider — The more a vendor knows about what you have in placetoday, the more they can highlight what they perceive to be the benefit they can bring above and beyond what you are doing today.
  4. 4. Known constraints — Anything else that a vendor should know that would help them self select themselves in/out of a dialogue with you will increase the chances of onlyspeaking with vendors who conceivably could be a fit.

Asking Good Questions

Ask ‘Em is designed help you get enough of an understanding about a vendor to make an informed decision as to whether it makes sense to take a call or meeting with them. As a director at a Fortune 50 CPG company once said: “I use Ask ‘Em to be able to gain sufficient enough awareness to: 1. understand what the vendor does, 2. understand how we might use them and 3. what key information I need to learn next to find out if we should prioritize engaging with them further.” Here are some good examples which you are free to use.

  • Please provide an overview of your service? What does it do?
  • Please provide an overview of your tech? How does your service do what it does?
  • What does it typically take to get up and running with your service? What inputs are required from us? What types of resources are typically required? How long does it typically take to implement?
  • What KPIs/metrics would a company like ours typically focus on improving by using your product?
  • Where/how would we measure your product’s impact on those KPIs?
  • Who do you compete with?
  • What is the biggest differentiation/advantage you have over your competitors?
  • Do you have any case studies that you can share with us?
  • Do you typically work with customers in our industry (e.g. Financial Services)?
  • What is an example of a company who is clearly very successful implementing a solution such as the one you provide - even if they are not your client? Help us see what we should be striving to achieve and surpass.
  • What is most basic, immediate use-case that a company like ours might pursue with your solution?
  • Once we get through a basic use-case, where might we go from there? What’s the more advanced user organization doing with your solution?
  • What question haven’t we asked you? How would you answer it?
  • Finally, what’s the vision look like? Describe what we might be doing/achieving in 3 years?
  • What is the most common misconception people have about your solution or yourcategory?
  • Do you integrate with X, Y or Z?
  • Do you have any key partnerships we should know about?
  • Can you describe your pricing model?

  • What’s this anonymity all about?

    Two of the most common reasons marketing and IT professionals give us for why they don’t want to reach out to a vendor that might be relevant to them are: 1. the fear of the onslaught of follow up that often comes w/ a vendor’s sales process and 2. they don’t want their existing vendor to find out they are shopping around. To remove this friction that prevents these valuable conversations from ever occurring, we allow verified brands to ask questions with an anonymous description (e.g. ‘Tier 1 Automotive OEM’) which gives the vendor enough information about their industry/sector but does not give out their specific contact information.
